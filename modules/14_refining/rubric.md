# Lab 14 Rubric: *Refining Data Visuals*

**Total weight:** 100% of the assignment grade  
**Grading philosophy:**

- Emphasis on thoughtful refinement decisions, not polish alone
- Reward evidence that the student is moving a real project graph forward
- Partial credit encouraged when the student shows coherent revision logic, even if the final chart is still rough
- Penalize generic answers that are not clearly tied to the submitted graph

---

## Weight Summary

| Question | Weight |
|:--|--:|
| Q1. Reflection on the guided refinement demo | 20% |
| Q2. Communication goal for the project graph | 15% |
| Q3. Diagnosis of the rough draft graph | 20% |
| Q4. Before/after refinement explanation and evidence of revision | 25% |

## Part 1: Guided Chart Refinement Demo

### Q1. Reflection on the guided refinement demo (20%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-:|------------|
| 90-100% | Clearly identifies two high-impact refinement moves from Steps 0-6 and explains, in specific terms, how those moves improved readability and made the message easier to see |
| 60-89% | Identifies two reasonable changes, but the explanation is somewhat generic, incomplete, or only partly connected to why the final version works better |
| 30-59% | Mentions at least one relevant change, but the response is vague, descriptive only, or does not clearly explain why the refinement mattered |
| 0-29% | Missing, off-topic, or shows little understanding of the refinement process |

**Key indicators:**

- Names concrete changes such as title/message, clutter removal, axis cleanup, legend removal, emphasis, or layout
- Explains why those changes help the audience read the chart faster
- Focuses on communication improvement rather than just listing edits

## Part 2: Refine One Graph for Your Project

### Q2. Communication goal for the project graph (15%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-:|--------------|
| 90-100% | Clearly states the decision or question, identifies the audience, explains what they should notice first, and provides a focused one-sentence takeaway tied to the submitted graph |
| 60-89% | Most elements are present, but one part is vague, underdeveloped, or only loosely connected to the graph |
| 30-59% | Gives some project context, but the audience, decision, or takeaway is unclear |
| 0-29% | Missing, generic, or not tied to a specific project graph |

**Key indicators:**

- Names a real project-related question or decision
- Identifies a plausible audience
- States what the graph is supposed to help that audience notice
- Gives a takeaway that could reasonably guide refinement choices

### Q3. Diagnosis of the rough draft graph (20%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-:|----------------|
| 90-100% | Gives a concrete diagnosis of the original graph, including at least two sources of clutter, one specific label/axis/legend/layout issue, what stands out first, and whether that matches the intended message |
| 60-89% | Identifies several real issues, but some observations are broad, incomplete, or not clearly linked to the intended message |
| 30-59% | Shows some awareness that the original graph has problems, but the diagnosis is thin, vague, or missing required elements |
| 0-29% | Minimal, generic, or missing diagnosis |

**Key indicators:**

- Refers to actual features of the submitted rough draft
- Distinguishes between visual clutter and communication problems
- Connects the diagnosis to message clarity, not just appearance

### Q4. Before/after refinement explanation and evidence of revision (25%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-:|----------------|
| 90-100% | Includes both original and refined graphs and gives a specific explanation of what changed and why. The revisions visibly improve emphasis, clarity, or audience focus, and the response shows real project progress and engagement with the refinement workflow |
| 60-89% | Shows meaningful revision and a reasonable explanation, but the changes are modest, the rationale is somewhat generic, or the link to audience/message could be stronger |
| 30-59% | Includes some revision effort, but the before/after difference is limited, the explanation is vague, or it is hard to tell how the changes help communication |
| 0-29% | Missing before/after evidence, little visible revision, or explanation is largely absent or off-topic |

**Strong answers usually do all of the following:**

- Use a real graph from the group project, or a plausible in-progress draft if the project is still developing
- Make visible changes rather than only describing intended revisions
- Explain how the refinements help the audience notice the main message more quickly
- Show engagement with the process even if the final chart is not fully polished

## Instructor / TA Notes

- Prioritize evidence of project progress and engagement over visual polish alone
